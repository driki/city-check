A FREE website review
 The website review below is customized for Middletown, NJ. Our mission is to make every city and town website an exceptional experience, one that is focused on the citizen, while also reducing effort for municipal employees.

  1. Try improving page load time to reduce user frustration.

     Your page load time for http://www.middletownnj.org is currently 739ms, which is pretty good, but users now expect an astounding 400ms response time, don't blink or your residents are likely to click away. When we reviewed your site we found 43 images, that totaled 220KB. Those extra bits and bytes in your images make a difference in how quickly your website loads. We're happy to tell you that your page load time was in the top 2% compared to the other municipal websites we've reviewed. We did not find HTTP Compression being used on your website. Compression can get your page content to the user faster, it's a simple way to increase speed and save on bandwidth.

  2. Consider putting fewer links on the page.

     When we reviewed your homepage we found over 138 links on the page. The good news is we only found 1 link that was broken, but lots of links means lots of choice and that much choice can confuse a resident or user. It also looks like you could benefit from having human readable URLs. A URL like http://www.middletownnj.org/press/read/17a6b8d4 doesn't help the user or a computer figure out what the content is on that page, whereas http://www.middletownnj.org/monmouth-county-fair-2013 does a much better job expressing what the user might find when following that link. Great examples of citizen focused websites are http://gov.uk and http://answers.honolulu.com, you'll notice how they organize content that is focused on how citizens think about their needs.

  3. Consider making contact information more prominent on the page.

     Good news! We did find basic contact information for your city. It took a little digging, but we found a phone number 732-615-2024 and a street address but unfortunately we couldn't find a general feedback email. Surprisingly, a Monmouth University study (PDF) found that only 20% of municipal websites have a generic email address. A single published phone number or email address in the footer can give residents a simple way to ask their question.

  4. Consider improving search instead of relying on navigation links.

     Make sure your site has a search function, and if possible use Google Site search. Your vendor CivicPlus offers search as part of their solution, but c'mon we're talking about Google. It'd be great to place it in a prominent location on your website. We found that your search box was only 100px wide by 16px tall. This is a pretty small search box, we recommend that a search box be no smaller than 200px wide by 20px tall. We also found that your search box is sort of buried on the page, 400px from the top, ideally your search box is located at the top of the page, users have come to expect it in the upper right hand corner or at least at the top of the page.

  5. Review your analytics to figure out what your residents want.

     Yippee! We found that you are using Google Analtyics to collect information about what people are viewing and how they are interacting with your website. Consider spending time reviewing citizen interactions with your website. Knowing when your residents need something can really help you craft a useful homepage. Often the information that residents are looking for is both periodic and episodic in nature. People generally don't look for winter parking ban information in July, whereas school registration information might be more useful in the spring. You can update your website content and navigation to reflect that.

  6. Try using Twitter or Facebook to communicate rather than broadcast information.

     Your residents use services like Facebook, Twitter, Tumblr and Pintrest, it looks like you are using Facebook and Twitter too. We reviewed your Twitter feed though, and it shows that you are mostly using it as a broadcast channel, only 2 of your recent Tweets are a conversation with someone. Twitter can be a great way to have a dialog with your residents. The Mayor of Newark, Corey Booker and Somerville, MA a great examples of municipalities using Twitter to communicate with residents.

  7. Consider a permissive robots.txt file to allow web crawlers.

     A robots.txt file is used by web crawlers and search engines like Google or Bing to add your website content to their search engines. We've reviewed yours and it looks great.

     Imagine improvements or new features being added to your website constantly. Changes made by talented engineers, without you having to pay for them.   9. Try removing images from your header so that people focus on your content.

     Yes, having beautiful photos of your city on your website might seem like a great idea, but most people who visit your website are there looking for a specific piece of information. The good news is that your website header is better than most, taking up only 105px. Consider setting up a unique promotional website for your town, one that is geared to attracting residents or new businesses. Your Chamber of Commerce or Economic Development office would be a good place for this information and brochure website. User ignore content-free pictures on websites. Local government websites are typically riddled with images like these.

  10. Consider publishing clear copyright, terms of service and a privacy policy.

     You can send your message with uncertainty trembling in your voice, or you can say it with confidence. If you're ending your messaging with question marks, using terms such as "perhaps", "maybe", "interested?" and "want to?", then most likely you have some opportunity to be a bit more authoritative. Who knows, maybe there is a bit more room for telling people what to do next in the world of conversion optimization.

  12. Try removing duplicate functionality from your site.

     We found 5 links that pointed to the same place.

  13. Try stating who the information is for instead of just presenting the information.

     There are primary user personas that interact with a municipal website: 1) Resident, 2) Municipal Employee, 3) Business 4) Legal staff, 5) Researchers. Consider informing the user on each page who this information is for. It's unlikely that someone who runs a small business is interested in how to register a child for public school, but a resident certainly would be.

  14. Try Exposing Options instead of hiding them.

     Your website navigation uses 'hover' to reveal additional menu items and choices. User research shows that hover menus can do more harm than good.

  15. Consider photos of employees reminding us that people work for the town.

     We mentioned earlier that photos on your website aren't helpful, but there are a certain type that are - photos of your employees. The Nielsen Norman Group research shows that people "like getting a sense of the company" when visiting their website. If there was ever an organization that could benefit from feeling a bit more personalized it's government. So, consider allowing your employees to add a casual photo, build that connection to the residents that might only know you via email.
